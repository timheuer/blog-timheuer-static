<p>i&rsquo;ve been reading a bit lately and have been a bit frustrating with some of the <a href="http://digg.com/programming/101_Reasons_Why_Java_is_Better_than_.NET">reading i&rsquo;ve found</a>.&nbsp; i like when people put microsoft in their place by pointing out factual evidence and apples-apples comparisons&hellip;or <a href="http://www.gotdotnet.com/team/compare/petshop.aspx">even provide specifications for us to build upon</a>.</p>
<p>one specifically that has been getting some attention is the article hosted on oracle.com with regard to <a href="http://www.oracle.com/technology/pub/articles/hull_asp.html">php vs. asp.net</a>.&nbsp; i&rsquo;m not even sure when this article was written &mdash; it has no timestamp information on it to understand the comparison time frame benchmarks.&nbsp; regardless, this article is being debated quite fervently in the asp.net *and* php communities, both claiming it favors both sides.</p>
<p><a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/joestagner/">joe</a>, a softie who works a lot with PHP, had <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/joestagner/archive/2006/05/01/587567.aspx">his own rebuttal today</a>.&nbsp; it seems that both sides are accurately frustrated.&nbsp; probably more so that it is written by a person that really introduces none of his credentials qualifying himself as an expert on either area, but as an oracle dba.</p>
<p>the article makes sweeping suggestions around security and speed &mdash; but provides no real data to back it up.&nbsp; if they are really true, i&rsquo;ll be the first one to stand in line as well and yell at my own organization &mdash; but you can&rsquo;t prove/fix that which isn&rsquo;t documented?&nbsp; you can&rsquo;t (or shouldn&rsquo;t be allowed to say in public and if you do, flogging allowed) say that &lsquo;it&rsquo;s faster&rsquo; and not put any meat behind that argument.&nbsp; heck, even lie about a test you did&hellip;but at least don&rsquo;t just put it there with a <em>because i say so</em> interpretation.</p>
<p>i&rsquo;ve also been dabbling in linux lately.&nbsp; trying to understand the passion behind it and the great distaste for windows.&nbsp; i really only have know windows intimately.&nbsp; i&rsquo;ve worked with linux a few times and at length on the server side (dns, etc.) but not really as a dev platform&mdash;and even that was bash-ing my way through the system with no gui &mdash; you know, the &lsquo;right&rsquo; way on linux ;-).&nbsp; so there&rsquo;s my caveat #1.&nbsp; caveat #2 &mdash; i have a mac.&nbsp; i like it.&nbsp; i think it can do things better than windows.&nbsp; i think windows does things better than it most of the time (especially with regard to my job/hobbies) &mdash; but more on that later.</p>
<p>anywhooo&hellip;.i received a copy of <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1590596277/sr=8-1/qid=1146520308/ref=pd_bbs_1/002-2892749-9996854?%5Fencoding=UTF8">Beginning Ubuntu Linux</a> and encouraged to look at it (as i&rsquo;ve bene looking more at fedora and suse).&nbsp; ubuntu is supposed to be the &lsquo;linux for real people&rsquo; operating system&hellip;so i decided to take a look.&nbsp; i got to page 9 before i was throwing up with the unsubstantiated goo in the book.&nbsp; i really would have loved to see a book about ubuntu&hellip;instead it starts out as a rant on &lsquo;why i hate microsoft&rsquo; veiled under a &lsquo;history of linux&rsquo; heading.</p>
<p>under a section entitled: The Benefits of Linux, a subsection of Crash-Free (note to editors, when you hyphenate, the second word isn&rsquo;t capitalized).&nbsp; first sentence verbatim: <em>&ldquo;A primary benefit of Linux is that it doesn&rsquo;t crash.&rdquo;</em>&nbsp; there aren&rsquo;t many sentences after that before it further states:&nbsp;&ldquo;<em>Of course, programs that run on top of Linux sometimes crash, but they don&rsquo;t take the rest of the system down with them&hellip;&rdquo;</em>&nbsp; and immediately below that there is a &ldquo;note&rdquo; of:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>&ldquo;Actually, very few programs under Linux crash.&nbsp; Because Linux programmers use a different method of bug testing than used by Microsoft developers, there are arguably fewer bugs, and those that are discovered are fixed very quickly.&rdquo;</p></blockquote>
<p>hmmm&hellip;really?&nbsp; this made me sick.&nbsp; why?&nbsp; i&rsquo;m not even going to argue if it is true or not&hellip;because i can&rsquo;t (if i was a new reader to the book).&nbsp; you can even apply the MLF (mother-in-law factor) design principle to book authoring.&nbsp; global comments like that are irresponsible if not backed up.&nbsp; if someone didn&rsquo;t know better, they take these comments as fact.&nbsp; this book provides no reference to facts/statements to support these comments &mdash; and that is irresponsible.</p>
<p>if i dissect the comment.</p>
<p>&ldquo;<em>&hellip;use a different method of bug testing than used by Microsoft&hellip;&rdquo;</em> &mdash; what method?&nbsp; and how do you know Microsoft developers don&rsquo;t use it?&nbsp; especially given our drive around test-driven development.</p>
<p>&ldquo;&hellip;<em>there are arguably fewer bugs&hellip;&rdquo;</em> &ndash; really, and how is that being measured?&nbsp; with windows are you bundling everything up, but with linux are you saying only the kernel, even though someone loaded it up with apache, php, mysql, postres, named, etc, etc. &mdash; bundle all them up, how many patches are there?&nbsp; and oh by the way, who has the responsibility of getting those updates to the consumer (note: i think redhat, etc. are getting <u>a lot</u> better and providing an auto update feature based on packages installed).&nbsp;</p>
<p>&ldquo;<em>&hellip;and those that are discovered are fixed very quickly.&rdquo;</em> &ndash; wow, based on what?&nbsp; if you look at a <a href="http://download.microsoft.com/download/9/c/7/9c793b76-9eec-4081-98ef-f1d0ebfffe9d/LinuxWindowsSecurity.pdf">forrester research paper</a> (posted on forrester for $775, but free here) that shows some interesting trends of time-to-fix of vulnerabilities.&nbsp; when using a definition (and i&rsquo;m only choosing not to define them here because you can get them online) of &lsquo;all days of risk&rsquo; (essentially vulnerability identified to the platform maintainer&rsquo;s first fix), microsoft has been substantially lower than the linux community.&nbsp; for the study period, microsoft had 25 days of risk, and (i&rsquo;ll use SUSE because the author keir thomas&rsquo; first book was beginning suse) SUSE was 74.&nbsp; the study gives credit to debian&rsquo;s developer federiation reducing the distribution days of risk.&nbsp; now granted the risk levels vary, but it does show that microsoft responds quickly.</p>
<p>there is also a comment in the book that linux is &ldquo;<em>far, far more secure&rdquo;<strong> </strong></em>than windows.&nbsp; take a look at the forrester study referenced in the previous section.&nbsp; during the study period microsoft had 128 total flaws, 86 of which were deemed high-security (67%), where SUSE had 176 identified flaws, 111 of which were deemed high-security (63%) &mdash; is that &ldquo;far, far more secure&rdquo;?&nbsp; not according to any known math.</p>
<p>at any rate, the book/article fueled frustration that when we talk about claims (on either side), we need to back them up with some level of facts.&nbsp; otherwise we aren&rsquo;t doing anyone a favor by spewing irresponsible opinion and the mouths of passionate zealots (microsoft ones included).</p>
